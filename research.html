Modeling Animal Behavior w/ AI

Vicario Lab Overview

"Songbirds use their songs and calls to communicate in social and reproductive contexts. They learn to make these sounds through a process of vocal imitation that has much in common with human speech acquisition. Very few animals are capable of this form of behavioral learning. It involves auditory discrimination, auditory memory and sensorimotor learning. We can study the brain mechanisms of each of these processes, because the relevant brain pathways have been identified in songbirds. Experiments in the laboratory involve a range of techniques from behavioral observations and sound processing to neurophysiology and neuroanatomy." - Vicario Undergraduate Research Lab Group Page

Research Items

Machine-learning Approach to Track Sound-Elicited Behavioral Changes [Poster]

  • Abstract: Discriminating between ethologically-relevant auditory stimuli is a fundamental ability that allows animals to appropriately navigate within their environment. Previous research with human infants suggests that this ability may be present early in development, since the introduction of novel sounds elicits unconditioned head-turning. Zebra finches (Zfs), a species of songbird, are often used to study auditory perception since they use communication signals to relay identity information and must therefore be able to differentiate between distinct acoustic signals. The assessment of auditory discrimination in ZFs has historically been approached by employing operant conditioining principles, such as response-consequence associations; this presents a problem, since ZFs must concurrently learn to differentiate sounds and task-demands related to the learning paradigm. The current study employed an ethological-analysis approach to determine if birds exhibited behavioral changes in response to changes to a predetermined auditory stream -- a modified version of an oddball paradigm, wherein a stream of repeating sounds (standards; the same ZF song) is presented and, occasionally, novel sounds (oddballs; Canary songs) are introduced. Behavioral changes (head movements) in response to auditory playback were tracked via cameras and a novel, 3D pose-estimation machine-learning algorithm (DeepLabCut, Matthis Lab) was used to automatically track ZF features (e.g. cheeks, eyes, beak, center-of-head); the resulting datasets were used to detect whether deviations in head movements correlated with the presentation of auditory stimuli. On average, oddball presentations elicited changes in head movements that were detected in a subset of predetermined measurements. The results suggest that tracking behavioral changes may provide a powerful tool for determining whether ZFs can discriminate between different sounds, specifically in an oddball-like playback paradigm. Additionally, a machine learning approach to tracking physical features of ZFs proved to be a sufficiently robust method that decreases the need of human-power that is typically associated in the analysis of camera-recorded behaviors.

Health Disparities in Medicine

Overview

Research is being conducted on women's health, infant mortality, maternal mortality, and the racial health disparities affecting these topics in New Jersey. The focus is on understanding the root causes of these problems and finding solutions that address the needs of the community. The research takes a grassroots approach, working closely with community organizations, healthcare providers, and policy makers to implement solutions that can improve the health and well-being of women and families in the state. The aim is to reduce infant mortality and maternal mortality rates, and address racial health disparities to create a healthier and more equitable future for all women and families in New Jersey.

Research Items

Implementation of the NSF I-Corps Entrepreneurship Training Approach to Decrease Racial Health Disparities in New Jersey via the Creation of a Maternal Healthcare Application

  • Abstract: The National Science Foundation (NSF) created the NSF I-Corps entrepreneurship training approach for academic researchers to bring their scientific innovations into the economic world by fixating on the demands of the potential consumer base, tackling difficulties in constructing a successful company, and a multi-disciplinary exploration of commercialization processes. With the maternal mortality rate for black mothers being 7 times higher than white mothers and the infant mortality rate for black infants being 3 times higher than white infants in New Jersey, the training approach - the “lean startup” methodology - was used to investigate the socioeconomic barriers that cause these disparities and contextualize technology’s roles in alleviating it. An AI predictive intervention application to prevent potential health consequences that a mother might encounter before/during/after the pregnancy process was used as the starting minimum viable product of the study and personal semi-structured interviews were conducted with minority mothers and healthcare providers. Key interview discussion points included but were not limited to trust in the healthcare system, technologies they used for their health, sources of pregnancy information, and issues with current pregnancy health apps. 27 interviews were conducted and 350 insights were collected to form a Business Model Canvas. Results consisted of heavy reliance but skepticism of Google by mothers, not enough education to make an informed decision, language barriers between the doctor and patient when communicating symptoms, inability to find and access a doctor, and how current apps do not focus on postpartum, individualized treatment, nor the mother’s social health. The findings indicate that symptom recorders, free maternal education, increased accessibility to doctors that speak the same language or of the same culture, and predictive health intervention are promising measures to address maternal health disparities.

Justice Health with University Correctional Health Care (UCHC)

Overview

University Correctional Health Care (UCHC) is a statewide program providing mental and physical health care for New Jersey’s inmate population.

Goals of Undergraduate Research Group:

  • Understand the unique benefits and challenges of working in correctional settings
  • Recognize key determinants of structural inequalities that may contribute to incarceration and medical diagnoses
  • Develop interpersonal skills by engaging in patient care alongside medical professionals
  • Identify evidence-based approaches to treatment of the common medical problems affecting justice-involved populations (i.e. mental illness, substance use disorders, and chronic illness)
  • Interpret the complexity of social justice issues from an advocacy and activism standpoint

Research Items

Preventing Sleep Deprivation in Correctional Facilities: The State of Current Knowledge and Recommendations [PRE PRINT]
  • Abstract: Studies that study the impact of psychological, physical (environmental), social, and cultural determinants on the sleep of inmates in correctional facilities are limited. Only some within these investigate prevention or treatment measures for sleep deprivation or insomnia and its respective efficacy. A literature review was conducted via PubMed, AccessMedicine, BioMed Central, and other resources using keywords such as "sleep and correctional facilities", "insomnia and correctional facilities", "sleep deprivation treatment", and other keywords relating to correctional facilities, sleep, and the treatment of different sleep disorders. Results consisted of a positive feedback loop between difficulties in sleep and mental disorder factors, prior litigation regarding sleep being an effective means in diagnosing infrastructure concerns and concentration of efforts of policymakers, cognitive behavioral therapy improving 70-80% of insomnia patients, and stimulus control and relaxation techniques reducing nightly awakenings in inmates. Results indicated that the Psychological Services Inmate Questionnaire, Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, and Multivariable Apnea Prediction Score are effective methods for calculating sleep quality in inmates, ensuring proper bedding, limiting internal conflicts between inmates, reducing blue light in prisons, and reassessing wake-up times is important to prevent Eighth Amendment violations, and that cognitive behavioral therapy is an effective treatment measure for reducing the symptoms of insomnia. More research needs to be conducted on the efficacy of hypnotic medication but the implementation of it with non-pharmacological treatments shows promising improvements in the sleep quality of inmates.
